# Keep Human

*A governance-oriented position statement for PAL LAB.*

> Keep Human is not anti-AI.
> It is the position that, as AI systems become more capable,
> the human role must be made more explicit rather than less.

-----

## 1. The Problem Is Not Capability

The dominant framing of AI risk is capability risk:
AI systems may become too capable, too fast,
in ways that humans cannot manage.

This framing is not wrong. But it is incomplete.

The deeper problem is not that AI will become capable.
It is that humans may become less explicit about
what they are responsible for — at precisely the moment
when AI capability makes that explicitness more necessary.

A highly capable AI system operating under
poorly defined human governance is not a governance solution.
It is an accountability gap at scale.

Keep Human is the position that human governance
must become more explicit as AI capability increases —
not less.

-----

## 2. AI Does Not Execute Human Input Directly

A foundational observation in this repository is that
generative AI systems do not execute human input directly.

Input is reconstructed, compressed, optimized,
and transformed before output is produced:

```
A → (A + C) → B′
```

Where A is human input, C is the reconstructive pressure
applied during inference — including optimization pressure,
training priors, compression, and constraint rewriting —
and B′ is the output.

C is not visible to the user.
C is not directly controllable.
C is the structural reason why outputs drift from intent,
why identical prompts produce different results,
and why governance cannot be reduced to
monitoring outputs after the fact.

This matters for Keep Human because it means
the human-AI relationship is not Input → Output.
It is Input → Reconstruction → Output.

The reconstruction layer is where intent is transformed.
Governing AI means governing that transformation —
not merely reacting to its results.

-----

## 3. Reconstructive Pressure and the Limits of Prompt Engineering

If C is the pressure that transforms input during
reconstruction, then the conventional response to
AI drift — improving prompts — is working at the
wrong layer.

Better prompts reduce the surface area through which
unwanted C can operate. They do not eliminate C.
They do not govern the conditions under which
reconstruction happens.

This is not a criticism of prompt engineering as a practice.
It is an observation about its scope.

Prompt engineering is a user-level intervention
on an inference-time process.
Governance is a structural intervention
on the conditions under which inference occurs.

Keep Human insists on the distinction.
Governance cannot be outsourced to prompt engineering
without losing something essential:
the explicit human authority to define what
reconstruction conditions are acceptable.

-----

## 4. Three Layers, in Order

The system should be understood as three layers:

**Layer 1: Human Governance Layer**
Humans define purpose, assign roles, set principles,
approve or reject outputs, and exercise stop authority.
This layer is not optional and cannot be delegated
to AI systems.

**Layer 2: Principle Layer**
The explicit principles, constraints, and norms
that govern how AI systems should behave.
These are authored by humans and must not be
silently rewritten by AI default behavior
or capability drift.

**Layer 3: Execution Layer**
The AI system operating within the conditions
established by Layers 1 and 2.

This order is not decorative. It is structural.

An AI system that operates in ways that implicitly
redefine principles — without human review or approval —
has inverted the intended hierarchy.
Execution has overwritten Principle.
Principle has displaced Governance.

Keep Human insists the order be maintained explicitly,
not assumed.

-----

## 5. What Humans Are For

A common framing of human value in an AI-heavy future
is residual: humans do what AI cannot yet do.
As AI capability expands, the residual shrinks.

Keep Human rejects this framing as incomplete.

Humans are not primarily defined by what AI cannot replicate.
Humans are defined as the origin of:

- **Meaning** — the purposes for which systems are built
- **Boundaries** — what the system is permitted to do
- **Responsibility** — who is accountable for outcomes
- **Governance** — the structure within which systems operate
- **Adoption and rejection** — what outputs are accepted
- **Stop authority** — the right to terminate a process
- **Role assignment** — who does what, including the AI

These functions are not contingent on AI capability.
They do not become less necessary as AI becomes more capable.
They become more necessary — because capable systems
operating without explicit governance produce consequences
at greater scale and speed.

-----

## 6. AI Capability Must Not Silently Rewrite Principle

A specific risk this repository documents is
what might be called principle erosion
through capability drift.

This occurs when:

- AI systems produce outputs that implicitly
  expand their own scope or authority
- Human operators accept those outputs without review
- Principles that were not explicitly revised
  are effectively revised by accumulated practice

This is not a dramatic event. It is a gradual one.
And it is facilitated by a related phenomenon
documented in this repository:

**Summary Assimilation** — the tendency of AI systems
to absorb immature but important distinctions
into familiar explanatory frames during summarization,
thereby reducing the visibility of unresolved problems.

When an AI system summarizes an emerging governance problem
as already-solved, and when that summary is accepted
without examination, governance has been transferred
to the system that was supposed to be governed.

Keep Human insists that:

- AI behavior should be explained and governed,
  not passively ratified.
- Human principles should not shrink to accommodate
  AI convenience or AI default behavior.
- The burden of justification runs from AI to human,
  not from human to AI.

-----

## 7. Connection to PAL LAB Research

Keep Human is not an abstract ethical position.
It connects directly to the research documented
in this repository.

**PAL and re-anchoring:**
PAL exists because identity does not persist automatically.
Re-anchoring requires explicit human decision —
what anchor to use, what constitutes an acceptable output,
when to abort. PAL supports re-anchoring.
It does not replace the human judgment that governs it.

**Persona layers:**
The persona layer hierarchy in this repository
places Governance PAL at the top.
This reflects the same structural claim as Keep Human:
governance must remain above execution,
and human authority must remain above both.

**Drift control:**
Identity drift, role drift, normative drift —
these are all cases where the execution layer
has drifted away from the principle layer.
Keep Human names the structural reason
why drift is a governance problem,
not merely a technical one.

**Principle–Execution Layer Separation:**
The three-layer model in Section 4 of this document
is the architectural expression of Keep Human.
Keeping the layers explicit and in order
is not bureaucracy. It is governance hygiene.

-----

## 8. What Keep Human Does Not Claim

Keep Human does not claim that:

- AI systems are inherently dangerous
- AI capability should be limited or slowed
- Human labor must be preserved against automation
- The future should resemble the past

It claims that:

- Human governance obligations do not diminish
  as AI capability increases
- The structure of governance must be
  maintained explicitly
- Accountability cannot be dissolved by capability
- The human role in defining purpose, setting principles,
  and exercising stop authority is non-negotiable

-----

## Status

This document is a working position statement.
It is intended to make explicit the governance
assumptions that underlie PAL LAB research —
not to replace that research with philosophical assertion.

The claims here are structural and governance-oriented.
They do not depend on validation of specific
empirical claims about AI systems.
They depend on the argument that explicit governance
is better than implicit governance —
and that capability without accountability
is not a governance solution.

> Keep Human is the position that humans must remain
> the governing authority over AI systems —
> not because AI cannot be capable,
> but because governance requires a subject,
> and that subject must be human.
