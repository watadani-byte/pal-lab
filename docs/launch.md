# PAL LAB — Launch Text

*Final version. April 2026.*

-----

## Short Launch Statement

PAL LAB is a public research and governance initiative focused on persistence, drift, and continuity in AI systems.

It began with a narrow operational problem: when a character or persona is established in one AI session, it tends to drift — across sessions, turns, and platforms. Reference images help. Prompt design helps. But neither, by itself, constitutes a governance framework.

PAL (Persistent Anchor Layer) and CIP (Character Identity Protocol) are attempts to describe and govern these conditions. Neither is a finished solution. Both are working frameworks under continued development.

As investigation continued, the problem widened. Persistence mechanisms that stabilize visual identity may also stabilize role, tone, and normative frame — for better or worse. That widening raised governance questions that visual continuity alone does not.

PAL LAB is where those connected questions are investigated together.

## Medium Version

PAL LAB is a public working space for investigating persistence, drift, and reconstruction continuity in AI systems — and for developing governance language adequate to those conditions.

It started from a specific and narrow problem.

Generative AI systems do not maintain identity automatically. Across sessions, turns, and platforms, character identity drifts — in visual features, in tone, in role, in cultural register. This is not a configuration error. It is the expected behavior of probabilistic reconstruction systems. The question is not how to prevent it entirely, but how to govern it.

PAL (Persistent Anchor Layer) is a proposed infrastructure concept: when validated anchor materials remain persistently available at inference time, reconstruction continuity across sessions may improve. This is an observational hypothesis. It has not been confirmed through controlled experimentation, and it is not vendor-endorsed.

CIP (Character Identity Protocol) is a governance framework built around that observation — defining anchors, identity validation gates, hard abort conditions, and re-convergence logic.

Both frameworks emerged from visual identity problems. But investigation revealed that the underlying issue is not only visual.

Persistence mechanisms that stabilize a character’s face may also stabilize role definition, behavioral register, and cultural framing — under the same inference-time conditions, through the same anchor materials. If that is true, then what is anchored carries governance obligations that go beyond image consistency. A contaminated anchor does not only produce visual drift. It may stabilize the wrong normative frame with equal effectiveness.

This widening — from visual identity to role, tone, normative frame, and contamination risk — is not an arbitrary theoretical leap. It follows from a straightforward reading of how reconstruction conditions work in generative systems.

PAL LAB is the place where these connected questions are documented, examined, and organized. Its purpose is not to claim final answers, but to make the questions governable — to give the problems names, frame the governance implications, and provide a public record of where the investigation stands.

Persistence without governance is not a solution.

## PAL LAB focuses on…

- Identity drift and continuity failure across AI sessions
- Anchor-bound reconstruction and the PAL hypothesis
- Role drift, normative drift, and culturally bounded reconstruction
- Contamination risk in persistent reference layers
- Governance language and frameworks for AI persistence mechanisms
- The relationship between continuity, stability, and accountability
- Documentation and validation framing for PAL, CIP, and related frameworks
